        \section{System Model and Practical Challenges in \texorpdfstring{\gls{ota}}{ }} A
        system that utilizes \gls{ota} computing can be modeled in various ways depending
        on the specific use case and propagation environment. Most architectures involve a
        set of \gls{ed}s that transmit concurrently to a \gls{fc} or \gls{es}. The
        communication channel is a critical component of this model, as it introduces
        physical impairments such as fading, noise, and path loss. In addition to the
        channel model, practical challenges inherent to wireless communications---such as
        time sampling errors, multipath propagation, and waveform design---can
        significantly degrade the computational reliability of \gls{ota} systems. To
        mitigate these effects, optimization strategies including power management,
        transceiver design, and equalization must be employed.

        \subsection{System Architecture and Channel Model}
            To mathematically formulate the impact of channel impairments on \gls{ota}
            computing, we consider a discrete-time network model consisting of multiple
            \gls{ed}s deployed in an agricultural environment. These devices utilize Pulse
            Amplitude Modulation (\gls{pam}) to transmit local temperature readings to a
            central \gls{fc}. 

        Let $x_i[n]$ denote the discrete-time signal transmitted by the $i$-th sensor. For
        an illustrative scenario involving two sensors, the transmitted sequences are
        defined as:
        \begin{align}
            x_1[n] &= [2, -1, 1] \\
            x_2[n] &= [1, 2, -1]
        \end{align}

        \subsubsection{The Ideal Target Function}
        The primary objective of \gls{ota} computing is to leverage the multiple access
        channel (MAC) superposition property to aggregate data natively in the air.
        Assuming a flat-fading, noise-free, and perfectly synchronized channel, the ideal
        target function received at the \gls{fc}, denoted as $s[n]$, is the direct sum of
        the transmitted signals:
        \begin{equation}
            s[n] = \sum_{i=1}^{K} x_i[n]
        \end{equation}
        where $K$ is the total number of transmitting devices. For the given sensor data,
        the ideal aggregated sequence is evaluated as $s[n] = [3, 1, 0]$.

        \subsubsection{Multipath Channel Distortion}
        In practical agricultural deployments, the physical environment exhibits multipath
        characteristics (e.g., reflections from terrain or machinery). This is modeled as
        a Finite Impulse Response (FIR) filter. Assuming a channel impulse response $h[n]$
        with a span of three taps, defined as $h[n] = [1.0, 0.5, -0.2]$, the actual
        received signal $y[n]$ is the convolution of the ideal aggregated signal with the
        channel response:
        \begin{equation}
            y[n] = s[n] * h[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{n} s[k] h[n-k]
        \end{equation}
        Evaluating this convolution yields the distorted sequence $y[n] = [3.0, 2.5, -0.1,
        -0.2, 0.0]$. The extension of the sequence length from three to five symbols
        mathematically illustrates the presence of Intersymbol Interference (\gls{isi}),
        wherein previous and future symbols bleed into the current sampling instance.

        \subsubsection{Mean Squared Error (MSE) Evaluation}
        To quantify the degradation caused by the multipath channel, the Mean Squared
        Error (\gls{mse}) is calculated between the ideal target function $s[n]$ and the
        actual received signal $y[n]$. The error at each discrete time step is given by
        $e[n] = y[n] - s[n]$. For a sequence of length $N$, the overall \gls{mse} is
        defined as:
        \begin{equation}
            \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} (y[n] - s[n])^2
        \end{equation}
        For the evaluated sequences, the discrepancy between the ideal sum and the
        ISI-corrupted signal results in an $\text{MSE} = 0.46$. This theoretical error
        establishes the baseline challenge that must be addressed through waveform design
        or digital equalization techniques at the \gls{fc}.

    \subsection{Practical Challenges}
        \subsubsection{Channel State Information (CSI) Imperfection}
        The theoretical model assumes perfect knowledge of the channel impulse response.
        In practice, the need for accurate and fresh \gls{csi} at transmitters or
        receivers is critical for precoding and equalization techniques
        \cite{sahin_survey_2023,yang_federated_2020,evgenidis_waveform_2025}. Obsolete or
        noisy CSI severely limits the ability to pre-distort signals, exacerbating the
        \gls{mse}.

        \subsubsection{Waveform and Filter Design}
        As demonstrated by the sequence expansion in the multipath model, standard digital
        communication transceivers suffer from time sampling errors and \gls{isi}.
        Designing custom pulse shapes, such as optimized Root Raised Cosine (RRC) filters
        or DNN-generated waveforms, is essential to force zero-crossings at the exact
        sampling instances, thereby suppressing ISI natively over the air
        \cite{evgenidis_waveform_2025}.

    \subsection{Optimization Strategies to Enhance OAC Performance}
        To mitigate the aforementioned challenges, several optimization frameworks have
        been proposed in the literature:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Power Management}: Strategies to mitigate aggregation errors
            involve optimizing power control policies at the \gls{ed}s to align received
            signal amplitudes, thereby minimizing the overall \gls{mse} or maximizing the
            convergence speed of federated learning algorithms
            \cite{sahin_survey_2023,cao_optimized_2022,evgenidis_waveform_2025}.
            \item \textbf{Transceiver Design}: The joint design of pre-processing
            functions at the transmitter and post-processing equalizers at the receiver
            allows the system to match the channel characteristics to the desired
            nomographic function, effectively canceling multipath echoes and minimizing
            computational distortion
            \cite{goldenbaum_robust_2013,hellstrom_optimal_2023,sahin_survey_2023,yang_federated_2020}.
        \end{itemize}
